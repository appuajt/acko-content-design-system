Content › Voice and tone

Voice and tone In progress

We're not a traditional insurance company that talks like a policy. We write for the human on the other end. So, no intimidating jargon or frustrating processes. We're here as a helping hand.

Voice — our personality

Voice is consistent. It's how we always sound, regardless of channel or context.

Tone — our mood

Tone shifts with context. It reflects the emotional weight of the moment.

Core voice principle

ACKO is trying to make insurance feel understandable, usable, and dependable when something goes wrong. So, we should sound:

  • Assuring
  • Plainspoken
  • Insightful, and
  • Transparent

When copy pulls in different directions, use this priority order to decide what wins:

Trust > clarity > usefulness > warmth > cleverness

Assuring

Insurance is tested when something goes wrong. That's when customers need ACKO to sound clear, steady, and in control.

How do we become assuring? By removing uncertainty, step by step. We explain what happened, what ACKO is doing, what the customer needs to do, and what happens next — clearly.

When to be more assuring

Customer is feeling: stressed, mid-claim, or something has just failed.

Examples: Claim updates, hospitalisation support, roadside assistance, document checks, payment confirmations.

When to be less assuring

Customer is feeling: neutral, browsing, exploring, comparing.

Examples: Landing pages, plan comparisons, product explainers, add-on education, pre-purchase journeys.

Too much reassurance here either feels like filler or, worse, plants doubt that wasn't there.

UseYour claim has been submitted. We'll review the details and update you by tomorrow.
AvoidWoohoo! Your claim journey has started.
Plainspoken

Insurance should not need a translator.

Plainspoken means saying what matters in words people understand: what's covered, what's not, what it costs, what to do next, and what could affect a claim. We make the important things easy to find, easy to read, and hard to misunderstand.

When to be more plainspoken

Customer is feeling: confused, time-pressed, cautious, trying to complete a task.

Examples: Quote flows, policy summaries, exclusions, claim forms, payment screens, error states, FAQs.

When to be less plainspoken

Almost never. Plainspoken is ACKO's default.

A term can be written in legal or regulatory language — but we should always explain it in a way customers can understand quickly and clearly.

Example: IDV (Insured Declared Value) — The current value of your car according to the insurer. If your car is stolen or damaged beyond repair, this is usually the maximum amount your insurer will pay you.

UseYou'll need to pay ₹2,000 because your policy has a deductible. ACKO will cover the remaining approved amount.
AvoidApplicable deductible shall be borne by the insured as stated in the policy.
Insightful

Being insightful means being the most informed person in the room — and using that knowledge to help the customer choose well, not to push them toward what we want to sell. Tell users something they didn't know they needed to know.

When to be more insightful

Customer is feeling: curious, deliberating, comparing, doing their homework.

Examples: Landing pages, plan comparisons, in-app educational content, pre-purchase WhatsApp nudges, policy explainers.

When to be less insightful

Customer is feeling: anxious, in crisis, time-pressed, mid-claim.

Examples: Claim communications, error states, emergency flows, payment failures.

In these moments, the customer doesn't need more information — they need the next step.

UseYour PUC expires in 3 days. Renew it now to avoid a fine.
AvoidYour PUC is about to expire soon. Take action immediately!
Transparent

No sugarcoating the tough stuff. Transparent means giving exclusions the same space as coverages — and putting both where the reader can see them. If it could catch someone off guard at claim time, say it upfront.

When to be more transparent

Customer is feeling: cautious, evaluating risk, reading fine print, deciding whether to buy.

Examples: Exclusions, waiting periods, deductibles, sub-limits, claim eligibility criteria, renewal terms, premium changes, refund policies, T&Cs.

When to be less transparent

If this question even came up in your head, go back and write it more transparently.

Use₹4,500 is not covered because consumables are not included in your policy.
AvoidCertain charges may be payable by you.

Content › Writing for surfaces

Writing for surfaces In progress

The same message lands differently on every surface. Match your register to where the reader is.

App UX / in-product

Functional writing. The reader is mid-task and needs to move. Every word should earn its place. If something is going wrong, say it clearly and immediately — no softening, no burying. Tone: calm and direct. Not warm, not cold. Just clear.

The UX copy principles that govern this surface: Check if copy is required → Be very clear → Be concise → Be useful → Be conversational → Be the brand. In that order.
Landing pages

The user is deciding whether to trust ACKO. Lead with what they need to know, not what ACKO wants to say. Anticipate the doubt and answer it before they have to ask. Be insightful — not just promotional.

DoAnswer the question the user is already asking — "Will this actually cover me?" — before they have to scroll to find it
AvoidLeading with ACKO's awards and recognitions before explaining what the product does
WhatsApp

Conversational but never casual. People expect a person here, not brand language. Every message should give the reader something useful — not just a nudge. Weave the user's name in organically, never at the start.

ElementRule
HeaderUnder 40 characters. Clear, compelling, context-setting. Makes the user want to read more.
Body400–500 characters ideal; under 100 words. More than 60 words requires scrolling.
First lineDon't open with "Hey [Name]". Bold the first line to grab attention. ~3 seconds to hook the user.
NameWeave it in organically — never at the start. "It doesn't get better, Kavita!" ✅ "Hi Kavita," ❌
Quick repliesUp to 3. More than 3 confuses the user.
Sign-offNo sign-off. No "Thanks, Team ACKO".
StructureLine 1–2: hook → Line 3–5: context/value → Remaining: details, benefits, CTA
Email

More room than other channels — but not an invitation to fill the space. The most important thing goes in the first two lines. Subject lines say the actual thing. Email is where policy updates, renewal reminders, and claim communications live — all high-stakes moments where plain, direct writing matters most.

ElementRule
Subject lineUnder 8 words — longer lines get cut off. Say the actual thing. Max 1 emoji.
Subject line focus"Payment successful for your plan" ✅   "Good news about your plan" ❌
CTAMust be in the first fold. Lets the user act immediately.
HeadingStart with "we" or "you". No names or personalisation in the heading.
Sign-offNo "Team ACKO" sign-off.
SubtextOptional. Skip if not needed.
UseSubject: Your policy expires in 3 days
UseHeading: You need to schedule a health check
AvoidSubject: We have great news about your insurance plan
AvoidHeading: Lab visit required (passive, impersonal)
Social media

Where ACKO's personality shows more than its principles. Wit and timing matter as much as voice. The one thing that doesn't change: don't oversell, and don't say things on social that can't be backed up everywhere else.

The voice can be playful and insightful on social — but stay consistent with the trust-first order. Never say something on social that would fail the Transparent or Assuring test in another channel.

Write the full term first, then the abbreviation in brackets. After that, the abbreviation alone is fine.

Writing for surfaces › UX writing › UX writing principles

UX writing principles In progress

Before writing a single word, run it through these six principles — in order. They're a filter, not a checklist.

1. Check if copy is required

If a design solution can replace copy and reduce cognitive load, don't write. Copy that explains a bad design is still bad design. Ask: can an icon, layout, or visual hierarchy do this instead?

DoUse a progress bar to show step position instead of "Step 2 of 4"
AvoidAdding a paragraph to explain what a clearly labelled toggle does
2. Be very clear

Write in complete sentences. Explain jargon the moment you use it. Avoid ambiguity — if a sentence can be read two ways, it will be read the wrong way. Clarity always beats brevity when they conflict.

UseYour deductible is ₹2,000 — this is the amount you pay before ACKO covers the rest.
AvoidApplicable deductible: ₹2,000
3. Be concise

Right level of detail — no more. No filler words, no throat-clearing, no restating what was already said. Pick the shorter word when both work equally well.

UseUpload your PAN card
Use"Use" not "utilise"
AvoidPlease upload a copy of your PAN card document in order to proceed
Avoid"In the event that" instead of "if"
4. Be useful

Every piece of copy should move the user toward their goal. Explain current status so they know where they are. Suggest the next action. If something goes wrong, tell them what to do — not just what happened.

UsePayment failed. Check your card details and try again, or use a different payment method.
AvoidPayment failed.
5. Be conversational

Use personal pronouns (you, your, we, our). Keep syntax simple. Active voice. Short sentences. Write the way a knowledgeable person would explain something to a friend — not the way a policy document would state it.

UseWe'll send you a reminder 3 days before your policy expires.
AvoidA reminder notification shall be dispatched to the insured 3 days prior to policy expiry.
6. Be the brand

Use brand-value references. Make positive associations only — with preparedness, transparency, and dependability. Not with excitement, fear, or pressure. If in doubt, ask: does this sound like ACKO, or does it sound like any insurance company?

UseYour documents are verified. You're covered from today.
AvoidCongratulations! You're now protected against life's uncertainties! 🎉

Patterns and components › CTAs

CTAs Complete

The surrounding copy carries the message. The CTA just confirms the action.

Write from the user's point of view

Write it as the user would think it, not as ACKO would instruct it. First-person works especially well at high-stakes moments like active claims.

UseSee my recommended plan
UseI want an update on my claim
AvoidAccess your plan recommendations
AvoidInitiate a claim update request
Keep it short

Six words max. Two or three is ideal.

UseRenew now
UseDownload policy
UseAdd vehicle
AvoidClick here to renew your policy
AvoidDownload a copy of your policy document
AvoidClick the button to add your vehicle
Sentence case, always

Exception: ACKO is always written in caps.

UseTransfer to ACKO
UseGet a quote
AvoidTransfer To ACKO
AvoidRENEW NOW
No punctuation

Apostrophes are the only exception.

UseLet's get started
UseCheck what's covered
AvoidGet started!
AvoidRenew now.
Use numbers for speed and effort

A number is more convincing than an adverb. Only use a time claim if the flow delivers it.

UseRenew in 2 mins
UseDone in 3 steps
AvoidRenew quickly
AvoidDone in minutes
Consistent verb conventions

Each verb has a specific use. Don't swap them freely.

VerbWhen to useExample
ViewSomething that already belongs to the userView policy, View breakdown
CheckA lookup or external queryCheck challans, Check vehicle info
ExploreIntroducing a product or serviceExplore car insurance, Explore Safe Drop
DownloadFetching a documentDownload policy, Download e-card
AddCreating or attaching something newAdd vehicle, Add nominee
Learn more / Know moreBoth acceptable — don't mix on the same screenLearn more about this plan
The right CTA for the moment

Some situations always call for the same CTA.

SituationCTA
You're explaining or informingGot it
Error, end screen, or information screenOkay
Multi-step flow, moving between stepsContinue
Destructive or irreversible actionConfirm what's being lost, not just the action — e.g. "Yes, cancel my policy"
Destructive actions: Name what will be lost, not just the action. "Yes, cancel my policy" not "Confirm".

Patterns and components › Headers and subheaders

Headers and subheaders In progress

The header carries the full weight of the screen. Write it so it can stand alone.

Sentence case, always

Capitalise only the first letter of the sentence. Use title case for proper nouns only — product names, plans, ACKO.

UseYour claim is under review
UseGet the ACKO Platinum Health Plan
AvoidYour Claim Is Under Review
AvoidGet The Acko Platinum Health Plan
No full stop at the end

Never end a page title, heading, or subheader with a full stop. Exception: if you are using two punchy short sentences for effect, use full stops.

UseUpload your PAN card
UseAlready have a plan? Switch to ACKO.
AvoidUpload your PAN card.
AvoidAlready have a plan? Switch to ACKO
Stay under 2 lines

3 lines is the absolute limit. A header that runs longer usually means two ideas are being forced into one — split them or simplify.

UseYour policy is active
AvoidYour policy has been activated and is now ready for you to use across all your healthcare needs
Don't depend on the subtext

The header should make complete sense on its own. If the meaning only works when you read the subtext underneath, rewrite the header.

UseWe need your Aadhaar to verify your identity
AvoidHeader: One more thing / Subtext: We need your Aadhaar to verify your identity
No user names or custom text in headers

Inserting a name changes the line length unpredictably and can break the meaning. Keep headers stable and generic.

UseYour policy renewal is due
AvoidHi Priya, your policy renewal is due
Use subtext sparingly

Subtext below a header adds context that didn't fit the header. If the information is critical, it belongs in the header. If no one would miss it, skip the subtext entirely.

UseHeader: Add a nominee / Subtext: They'll receive the payout if a claim is approved
AvoidSubtext: ACKO requires you to nominate a person who will receive the claim amount in the unfortunate event of your passing

Patterns and components › Body copy

Body copy In progress

Body copy adds context below the header. If the header already does the job, skip it.

One message per sentence

Combining two ideas in one sentence makes both harder to absorb. Say one thing, then stop. Start a new sentence for the next idea.

UseYour cashless request has been submitted. We'll send an update within 2 hours.
AvoidYour cashless request has been submitted and we will review it and send you an update soon.
Use full sentences

Fragments feel incomplete and often force the user to infer meaning. Write in full sentences — subject, verb, outcome.

UseWe need this to verify your identity before processing the claim.
AvoidFor identity verification and claim processing purposes.
Active voice, second person

Write in active voice. Always address the user as "you". Never use "customer", "policyholder", "insured", or "claimant" unless legally required.

UseYou'll receive the payout within 5 business days.
UseWe've updated your policy.
AvoidThe payout will be received by the policyholder.
AvoidThe policy has been updated.
Highlight key information

Bold amounts, dates, deadlines, and document names when they appear inline. Don't bold decoratively — only use it for information the user needs to act on or remember.

UseYour premium of ₹4,200 is due by 15 June.
AvoidYour premium is due soon. Please pay it.
Lead with the user's outcome

Start with what happened or what the user gets — not with ACKO's process. The user's outcome is what they care about first.

UseYour claim is approved. ₹12,000 will be transferred to your bank account.
AvoidWe have completed our review process and are pleased to inform you that your claim has been approved.

Patterns and components › Empty states

Empty states In progress

Empty states are not errors — don't apologise for them. State what's missing and give the user something to do next.

State what's absent, not what went wrong

An empty state is usually neutral — nothing has failed. Don't frame it as an error. Tell the user why the space is empty and what they can do.

UseNo policies added yet. Add a vehicle to get started.
UseNo claims filed. Your claim history will appear here.
AvoidOops! Nothing to see here.
AvoidSorry, you don't have any claims yet.
First-time vs. cleared states

A first-time empty state needs more context — the user doesn't know what this section is for yet. A cleared state (e.g. all notifications read) needs less.

StateWhat to sayExample
First-timeExplain the section + invite action"No documents uploaded. We'll ask for documents when you file a claim."
ClearedConfirm and optionally invite action"You're all caught up. No new notifications."
Filtered / no resultsExplain the filter + offer a next step"No claims match this date range. Try a different period."
Give a next step where it makes sense

If there's an obvious action the user can take to fill the state, include it. If the state is genuinely passive (nothing to do), don't force a CTA.

UseNo saved addresses. Add one for faster checkout.
AvoidNo claim history yet. Explore our plans!

Patterns and components › Error states

Error states In progress

Errors are already stressful. The copy should reduce that stress — not add to it. State what happened, why, and what to do next. Then stop.

Don't blame the user

Frame errors around what happened, not what the user did wrong. Blame creates friction and damages trust.

UseThese details don't match our records. Please check and try again.
UseWe couldn't verify your PAN. Please upload a clearer photo.
AvoidYou entered incorrect details.
AvoidYour photo is too blurry.
No "Oops" — stay calm

Words like "Oops!", "Uh oh!", or exclamation marks make errors feel more alarming. Use calm, direct language instead.

UsePayment unsuccessful. Please check your card details and try again.
AvoidOops! Something went wrong with your payment!
Always give a recovery path

Every error message must tell the user what to do next. An error with no next step leaves the user stranded.

UseWe couldn't load your policy details. Pull down to refresh, or contact us if this keeps happening.
AvoidSomething went wrong.
Deliver bad news plainly

Get to the point. Framing bad news with softening preamble ("We have some unfortunate news to share with you…") delays the information and reads as evasive.

UseWe couldn't approve your cashless request. Your documents were outdated.
AvoidIt may be disheartening but your cashless request was not approved.
Use "sorry" only when it's ACKO's fault

Don't apologise for user errors, network issues, or things outside ACKO's control. When ACKO is genuinely at fault, say sorry — but don't follow it with "but".

UseSorry, we couldn't cancel your medical tests — the doctor was unavailable.
AvoidSorry for the delay, but no agents are available right now.
Rule: Never follow "sorry" with "but". It cancels the apology.

Patterns and components › Success states

Success states In progress

Confirm what happened, then tell the user what happens next. Positive but composed — not a celebration.

Confirm, then give the next step

A success state has two jobs: confirm what happened and tell the user what to expect next. Don't stop at confirmation alone.

UseYour claim has been submitted. We'll review the details and update you by tomorrow.
UsePayment successful. Your policy is now active.
AvoidWoohoo! Your claim journey has started.
AvoidPayment done!
Claims and emergencies: stay composed

When the context involves a claim, hospital, accident, or emergency — keep the tone calm and controlled. No exclamation marks, no emojis, no over-celebration.

UseYour cashless request is approved. The hospital will be notified within 30 minutes.
AvoidGreat news! 🎉 Your claim is approved!
Include timelines and specifics

Specific timelines reduce follow-up anxiety. Tell the user when they'll hear back, when money will arrive, or when the policy kicks in. Vague language creates doubt.

Use₹12,000 will be credited to your bank account within 3–5 business days.
AvoidThe amount will be transferred shortly.
What to include in a success state
ContextWhat to confirmWhat to add
Policy purchasePolicy is activePolicy number, document download link
Claim submissionClaim is receivedClaim ID, expected timeline, next step
PaymentPayment successfulAmount, transaction ID, what it covers
Profile updateChange savedWhat changed — only if non-obvious

Patterns and components › Alert and nudge cards

Alert and nudge cards In progress

Alerts look out for the user before a risk becomes a loss. The formula is simple: trigger + consequence + action. Helpful, not alarmist.

Trigger + consequence + action

An alert that only states a trigger ("Your PUC is expiring") gives the user nothing to act on. Include the consequence so they understand why it matters, then give the action.

UseYour PUC expires in 3 days. Renew it now to avoid a fine.
UseYour policy renews on 15 June. Pay now to avoid a lapse in coverage.
AvoidYour PUC is about to expire soon. Take action immediately!
AvoidYour policy is expiring.
Use exact numbers, not approximations

Specific numbers are more actionable than vague ones. "3 days" creates a deadline. "soon" creates vague unease.

Use₹2,500 is due in 2 days.
AvoidA payment is due very soon.
Helpful, not alarmist

Alerts should create a sense of usefulness, not panic. Don't overstate risk or use fear as the hook. State the risk plainly and give the user agency.

UseYour motor policy expires in 7 days. Renew to stay covered.
AvoidWarning! Your policy is expiring! You're at risk of huge fines and financial loss!
Tone by alert type
TypeToneExample
Renewal reminderHelpful, direct"Your policy renews in 5 days. Renew now to avoid a gap."
Compliance deadlineFactual, specific"Your PUC expires tomorrow. Renew to avoid a ₹2,000 fine."
Claim updateCalm, assuring"We need one more document to process your claim."
Nudge / upsellPlainspoken, not pushy"Your plan doesn't cover maternity. Add it before your next renewal."

Patterns and components › Loading states

Loading states In progress

Tell the user what's happening and how long it will take. Vague loading copy creates anxiety. Specific copy creates trust.

Name what's happening

Generic loading messages tell the user nothing. Name the specific action so they know the system is working on the right thing.

UseWe're checking your documents. This usually takes up to 2 hours.
UseVerifying your payment details…
AvoidProcessing your request…
AvoidPlease wait…
Give a time estimate

If a process takes more than a few seconds, tell the user how long. A specific estimate is more reassuring than silence or vague language.

UseYour claim is being reviewed. We'll update you within 24 hours.
AvoidYour claim is being processed. Please wait.
Use "please" when asking the user to wait

"Please" softens an instruction when the user is being asked to do something inconvenient — like wait. Use it here, but don't use it as a filler elsewhere.

UsePlease wait while we look for an agent.
AvoidPlease check your premium details
Loading copy by wait length
Wait timeWhat to showExample
Under 3 secondsSpinner + action name"Verifying details…"
3–30 secondsAction name + context"Confirming your payment. This takes a few seconds."
Minutes to hoursAction + specific timeline + what happens next"We're checking your documents. This usually takes up to 2 hours. We'll notify you when it's done."

Patterns and components › Modals and bottom sheets

Modals and bottom sheets In progress

Modals interrupt the user's flow. Use them to explain or confirm — not fill space. Always give a clear exit.

Bottom sheets explain — they don't decorate

Bottom sheets should clarify something on the current screen or confirm an action the user is about to take. Don't use them to host primary flows or replace full screens.

UseA bottom sheet explaining what "co-pay" means after the user taps a help icon
UseA confirmation sheet before a destructive action: Are you sure you want to cancel your policy?
AvoidA bottom sheet with a full multi-field form
AvoidA bottom sheet that takes up more than half the screen
Keep it under half the screen

If the content needs more than half the screen, it's too much for a bottom sheet. Move heavy content into info boxes, bullet points, or a dedicated screen instead.

If a bottom sheet is content-heavy, break the copy into structured elements — short header, bullet points, or info cards — rather than long paragraphs.
Informational sheets always use Got it

Patterns and components › Disclosure and helper text

Disclosure and helper text In progress

Helper text guides the user through a field. Disclosures make the catch visible. Both should read like a person wrote them — not a legal team.

Answer the field question

Helper text sits below or inside a field and tells the user exactly what format or value to enter. Keep it to one short sentence. It disappears once the user types — so front-load the useful information.

UseEnter your 10-digit mobile number
UseDate of birth in DD/MM/YYYY format
AvoidPlease enter your mobile number here
AvoidThis field is mandatory
Placeholder text is not helper text

Placeholder text disappears when the user starts typing — so it can't carry instructions the user might need mid-entry. Use it only for examples of valid input, not for rules.

UsePlaceholder: Eg: 9876543210 + Helper: Enter your 10-digit mobile number
AvoidPlaceholder: Enter your 10-digit mobile number without spaces or dashes
Make the catch visible

Exclusions, waiting periods, deductibles, and co-pays must be stated clearly — not buried in small text at the bottom. If a user needs to know it before they decide, put it where they'll see it.

UseThis plan has a 30-day waiting period for pre-existing conditions.
UseYou'll pay ₹2,000 as deductible. ACKO covers the rest.
Avoid*Applicable terms and conditions apply
AvoidSubject to policy wording
Always explain why

When asking for sensitive information — PAN, Aadhaar, health details, permissions — explain why before the user questions it. A one-line reason reduces drop-off.

UseWe need your PAN to process the claim payout to your account.
UseLocation access helps us find nearby network hospitals.

Standards › Grammar and style

Grammar and style In progress

Rules that keep ACKO's writing personal, active, and direct.

Write directly to the reader

Write directly to the user. Use "you" and "your". Never use "customer", "policyholder", "insured", or "claimant" unless legally required.

UseYour policy starts today
AvoidThe customer's policy starts today
Refer to ACKO as "we"

ACKO always refers to itself as "we" and "our" in product and customer-facing copy. Never use "ACKO" to refer to the company in the same sentence as "you" — it creates distance.

UseWe'll update you by tomorrow
AvoidACKO will update you by tomorrow
Use contractions

Contractions make writing sound human. Avoid them only in legal, regulatory, or formal disclosure copy where precision is required.

UseWe'll update you soon
UseYou don't need to do anything
AvoidWe will update you soon
AvoidYou do not need to do anything
Write in active voice

Active voice is clearer and shorter. Passive voice hides who is doing what — which creates ambiguity and erodes trust. A quick test: insert "by zombies" after the verb. If the sentence still works, it's passive.

UseWe're reviewing your claim
UseACKO will transfer ₹12,000 to your account
AvoidYour claim is being reviewed (by zombies ✓ = passive)
Avoid₹12,000 will be transferred to your account
Exception — status and progress screens: When the user is checking the status of something they've submitted, describe the state of their item — not the action. Your claim is under review tells the user where things stand. Your claim is being reviewed is passive and hides who's responsible. State descriptions are not the same as passive voice.
Use British English spelling

Use British English spelling. Indian schools follow British conventions, and it aligns with how most users read and write.

Use (British)Avoid (American)
CancelledCanceled
BehaviourBehavior
AuthoriseAuthorize
OrganiseOrganize
ColourColor
Licence (noun)License
Use "please" purposefully

Use "please" purposefully — to soften a message, when asking someone to wait, or when asking for extra effort. Don't use it as a filler or to sound polite in contexts where it adds nothing.

UseWe couldn't process your cashless claim. Please submit a reimbursement claim instead.
UsePlease wait while we look for an agent.
AvoidPlease check your premium details
AvoidPlease review the documents you've uploaded
Apologise only when it's our fault

Apologise only when it's ACKO's fault. An apology doesn't always require the word "sorry" — acknowledge the situation and move to the solution. Never follow "sorry" with "but".

UseSorry, we had to cancel your medical tests — the doctor was unavailable.
UseThis usually doesn't take this long.
AvoidSorry for the delay but no agents are available right now.
AvoidSorry, your documents don't match our records.
Rule: Never follow "sorry" with "but". It cancels the apology.
Lead with the bad news

Get to the point. State the fact, the cause, and the user's option. Don't build up to bad news with softening preamble — it reads as evasive and delays the information the user needs.

UseWe couldn't approve your claim. Your documents were outdated.
AvoidWe have some bad news. It may be disheartening but your claim is rejected.

Localisation and translation

What transfers directly, what needs cultural adaptation, and how tone shifts across markets.

Write for translation first

Every string you write in English should be easy to translate. That means: one idea per sentence, active voice, no idioms or metaphors that don't travel well, and no cultural references that assume a shared context. If a sentence is hard to read in English, it'll be harder to translate.

DoYour premium is due on 10 July.
DoUpload your ID to continue.
AvoidYour renewal is just around the corner — don't let it slip through!
AvoidDon't forget to get your ID in before we can move things forward.

Translated strings are almost always longer than English. Hindi can expand by 20–30%, Kannada's script is visually denser, and European languages like German or French regularly run 30–40% longer. Never write UI copy that fills every pixel. If a label or heading looks tight in English, it'll break in translation.

How we approach life insurance

Use gender neutral language

ACKO writes to users in second person: "you" and "your". When a third party comes up — a nominee, a doctor, an agent — keep it gender neutral. Don't default to "he" just because a role sounds gendered.

If you don't know someone's gender, restructure the sentence so a pronoun isn't needed. If that's not possible, "they", "them", and "their" work. If you're writing about a specific person, use the pronouns they go by. When in doubt, use their name.

DoIf your doctor recommends surgery, they'll need to certify it.
DoYour nominee will receive the claim amount.
AvoidIf your doctor recommends surgery, he'll need to certify it.
AvoidYour wife will receive the claim amount.
Don't assume a family structure

Insurance copy constantly references family. Use "the people who depend on you", "your family", or "your loved ones" — not assumptions about who that is.

DoCover the people who depend on you.
DoAdd up to 6 family members to your health plan.
AvoidProtect your wife and kids.
AvoidCover your husband, wife, and children.
